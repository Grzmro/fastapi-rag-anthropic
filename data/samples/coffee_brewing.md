# A Practical Guide to Brewing Coffee

## The golden ratio

A widely used starting point is the "golden ratio" of 1 to 17 — that is, one
gram of coffee for every 17 grams of water. For a standard mug of about 300
milliliters of water, this works out to roughly 18 grams of ground coffee.
Adjust to taste: less coffee for a milder cup, more for a stronger one.

## Grind size

Grind size has a large effect on flavor. A coarse grind, similar to sea salt,
suits the French press. A medium grind works well for drip and pour-over
methods. A fine grind, close to table salt, is used for espresso. Grinding
just before brewing preserves aroma, because ground coffee loses freshness
within minutes.

## Water temperature

The recommended brewing temperature is between 90 and 96 degrees Celsius.
Water that is too hot extracts bitter compounds, while water that is too cool
produces a sour, under-extracted cup. If you do not have a thermometer, let
boiling water rest for about 30 seconds before pouring.

## Pour-over method

Rinse the paper filter with hot water first to remove any papery taste and to
preheat the vessel. Add the grounds, then pour a small amount of water to wet
them evenly and wait 30 seconds — this stage is called the "bloom" and lets
trapped carbon dioxide escape. Continue pouring in slow circles. The whole
brew should take about three to four minutes.

## Storage

Store coffee beans in an airtight container away from light, heat, and
moisture. Do not keep beans in the freezer for daily use, as repeated
temperature changes cause condensation. Whole beans stay fresh for about four
weeks after roasting; ground coffee is best used within two weeks.
